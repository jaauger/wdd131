Federal Solar Tax Credit

Active (30%)

Officially known as the **Residential Clean Energy Credit**, this incentive is a dollar-for-dollar reduction in your federal income taxes owed.

  • **Credit Amount:** 30% of total system cost (no maximum limit).
  • **Eligibility:** Systems installed and placed in service by the end of 2025 qualify for the 30% rate. The credit is available for primary or secondary U.S. residences.
  • **How to Claim:** File [IRS Form 5695]() with your federal tax return. Keep all receipts and manufacturer certifications.
  • **Important Note:** The credit is nonrefundable but can be carried forward to future tax years if it exceeds your tax liability.

System Cost:

The costs of a typical residential solar system can can be broken down into the following:

  • Solar Panels, Inverters & Hardware (~ $2.50–$3.50 per watt) — For a typical residential system, this works out to roughly $15,000–$25,000 before incentives.
  • Average System Cost for a Medium-Size Home (~ 6–8 kW) — A 6 kW–8 kW system typically costs around $16,800–$26,400 before incentives.
  • Installation Labor, Permits & Soft Costs — These non-equipment costs (labor, permitting, mounting, wiring, etc.) are built into the per-watt pricing and typically represent a significant portion of the total; smaller or more complex installs may increase soft costs per watt.
  • Net Cost After Incentives — With typical incentives (for example, the federal tax credit), many homeowners pay significantly less than the sticker price: this makes the upfront cost more manageable compared to the pre-incentive amount.

Long-Term Savings:

Many homeowners see a dramatic reduction in electricity bills and often reach a break-even point within 6–10 years.

  • Reduce Your Monthly Utility Bill — Lower or eliminate electricity costs for 25+ years.
  • Lock In Predictable Energy Prices — Avoid rising utility rates with stable solar energy costs.
  • Increase Home Value — Solar-equipped homes often sell for more and attract more buyers.
  • Earn Credits Through Net Metering — Get credit for excess energy your system sends back to the grid.
